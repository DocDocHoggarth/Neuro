Station 1

Dorsal vs Ventral Roots

Start with the directional logic of spinal root traffic before layering in clinical patterns.

Badge available: Root Foundations
Learning target Differentiate the structure and function of dorsal roots, ventral roots, and the mixed spinal nerve that forms when they join.

Core idea

The dorsal root carries afferent sensory fibers into the spinal cord. The ventral root carries efferent motor fibers out of the spinal cord toward muscles and glands. When they join, they form a mixed spinal nerve that contains both sensory and motor fibers.

Common Mix-Up

A dorsal root is not the same as a dorsal ramus. This station stays focused on the root-level entry and exit pattern before the spinal nerve branches further.

Station 2

Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG)

Now identify the sensory cell body station that sits just outside the cord on the dorsal root.

Badge available: DRG Recognition
Learning target Identify the location and significance of the dorsal root ganglion in sensory transmission.

Clinical anatomy

The dorsal root ganglion contains the cell bodies of sensory neurons. It lies outside the spinal cord along the dorsal root. Sensory information moves through this region before entering the cord, so irritation or compression near the intervertebral foramen can create radiating pain, paresthesia, or numbness.

Clinical Relevance

A lesion near the intervertebral foramen can mechanically irritate the dorsal root and nearby DRG, helping explain radiating sensory symptoms into the limb.

Reflection prompt

Before opening the checkpoint, predict which structure near the intervertebral foramen is most likely to be involved when a patient reports radiating sensory symptoms, and explain your reasoning in one sentence.

Station 3

Segmental vs Peripheral Nerve Innervation

Shift from “single root” thinking to the clinically different pattern of a named peripheral nerve.

Badge available: Pattern Analysis
Learning target Differentiate segmental innervation from peripheral nerve innervation and explain why the resulting clinical patterns differ.

Segmental or root-level pattern

Distribution from a single spinal level. Think in dermatomes, myotomes, and reflexes associated primarily with one root.

  • C6 sensory change can affect the lateral forearm and thumb.
  • A root lesion may influence several named peripheral nerves that share that root.
  • The pattern often feels “root based,” not “named nerve based.”

Named peripheral nerve pattern

Distribution within a single named peripheral nerve that may contain fibers from more than one spinal level because of plexus mixing.

  • Radial, median, and ulnar nerves each contain mixed segmental fibers.
  • A lesion follows the named nerve’s sensory and motor territory.
  • The deficit may not match one clean dermatome or one clean myotome.

Station 4

Plexus Reorganization

Track how fibers move from roots into the mixed spinal nerve, through rami, and into named peripheral nerves.

Badge available: Plexus Integration
Learning target Explain how multiple spinal segments reorganize in a plexus to form a single peripheral nerve.

Concept pathway

The dorsal and ventral roots join to form a brief mixed spinal nerve. That spinal nerve then divides into rami. In limb regions, the ventral rami interweave into a plexus. Fibers are redistributed again into named peripheral nerves.

Key Takeaway

When weakness is reduced rather than absent, ask whether more than one spinal root may contribute to the movement being tested.

Station 6

Dermatomes and Myotomes

Use sensory territories and characteristic movements to localize findings at the root level.

Badge available: Clinical Localization
Learning target Define dermatomes and myotomes, and use them to distinguish root lesions from peripheral nerve lesions.

Localization language

A dermatome is a sensory territory supplied primarily by one spinal nerve root. A myotome is a muscle group or characteristic movement supplied primarily by one spinal nerve root. These concepts matter because root lesions often produce a dermatomal or myotomal pattern, while peripheral nerve lesions often follow a named nerve distribution instead.

Practice frame

  • Use upper-limb sensory findings to decide whether the pattern fits a dermatome or a named nerve territory.
  • Use lower-limb weakness patterns to decide whether the deficit fits a myotome or a more distal peripheral nerve problem.
  • Ask whether the findings align with one root-level pattern or with a named nerve distribution.

Clinical distinction

If sensory loss and weakness align with one root-level pattern, think radiculopathy or root lesion. If they line up with one named nerve distribution, think peripheral nerve lesion.

How to Localize

Use this decision guide after you finish the stations. It turns the full lab into one clinical reasoning sequence you can reuse when you see sensory change, weakness, or mixed limb findings.

Step 1: Identify the type of finding

Start by asking what kind of deficit you are seeing.

  • If the finding is primarily sensory, think about dorsal root input and whether a dermatomal pattern is present.
  • If the finding is primarily motor, think about ventral root output and whether a characteristic movement is weak.
  • If sensory and motor findings are both present, keep both the root and named peripheral nerve possibilities open.

Step 2: Decide whether the pattern is root-level or named-nerve

Then ask whether the findings fit one spinal level or the territory of one named peripheral nerve.

  • A dermatome or myotome pattern points you toward a spinal root or segmental problem.
  • A named sensory or motor territory points you toward a peripheral nerve lesion.
  • If the pattern crosses more than one named nerve but still looks segmental, a root lesion becomes more likely.

Step 3: Remember plexus mixing before you over-localize

Do not assume one weak movement equals one single root or one single distal nerve without checking the pattern.

  • Fibers from multiple spinal levels mix in a plexus before they enter a named peripheral nerve.
  • That means one root lesion may weaken a movement without eliminating it completely.
  • It also means one named peripheral nerve can contain fibers from several spinal levels.

Step 4: Use simple anchor examples

Return to the anchor examples from the lab before jumping to a diagnosis.

  • Lateral forearm and thumb sensory change fits a C6-type dermatome pattern.
  • Ankle dorsiflexion weakness fits an L4-L5-type myotome pattern.
  • Symptoms isolated to a named nerve territory fit a peripheral nerve pattern more than a root-level pattern.

Quick Decision Path

1. Is the finding sensory, motor, or mixed? 2. Does it fit one spinal level or one named nerve territory? 3. Could plexus mixing explain why weakness is partial rather than complete? 4. Which dermatome or myotome anchor best fits the exam findings?

Quick review by concept

  • Dorsal roots bring afferent sensory information into the spinal cord. Ventral roots send efferent motor output away from the spinal cord.
  • The dorsal root ganglion sits on the dorsal root outside the spinal cord and contains sensory neuron cell bodies.
  • Segmental patterns point toward a spinal level. Named peripheral nerve patterns reflect plexus-based mixing from multiple levels.
  • Ventral rami interweave in the cervical, brachial, lumbar, and sacral plexuses before fibers redistribute into named nerves.
  • Dermatomes are sensory territories. Myotomes are characteristic movement or muscle-group patterns. Both support localization.
